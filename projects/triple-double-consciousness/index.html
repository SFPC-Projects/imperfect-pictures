Triple-Double Consciousness

Silas Munro

In these Jacquard-woven textiles, I created a self-portrait with uncanny resonances and resemblances between myself and Du Bois. On one side of the diptych, I superimposed my own face into a well-known photograph of Du Bois at the 1900 Paris Exposition, where he first exhibited his signature data portraits. The title of the work—like that of the exhibition it is in—references Du Bois’s concept of double consciousness, the racialized sensation and unreconciled striving of looking at and measuring oneself through the eyes of others. Triple consciousness extends this concept even further, underscoring how race inevitably intersects with gender and sexuality. I’ve always been fascinated this particular portrait of Du Bois and how it presents Du Bois as a Black Dandy. I have written about his data visualizations, and feel close to him. In reproducing his image, I thought about his term double consciousness where Black people have this two ways of seeing them selves, and how for me as queer Black man, there is another layer of consciousness on top of that a kind of “triple consciousness.” To me Du Bois is queered by my presence, and the history of Dandies, In doubling this image and flipping it—thinking of Hito Steyerl’s “poor image” translated into textiles where there is both a shadow and a lowering of resolution, but a deepening of meaning when we are shown in mirror of each other. The Triple Double is a term in Basketball when a player in basketball a double digit total in three of the five main statistical categories. This double entendre about data point for the hyper masculine sport that I don’t play also is playing up this contrast between us. Historical accounts of Du Bois point of view of queerness are mixed. There is record of him being critical of a queer contemporary Alain Locke, but also rumors and hearsay about his own closet queerness.
